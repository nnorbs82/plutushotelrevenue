Hotel Photoshoot Tips: How to Get the Best Shots
In today’s visually driven world, a single photograph can speak volumes about a destination, setting the tone for a guest’s entire experience before they even set foot in a hotel. Whether you’re a hotelier looking to promote your property, a travel blogger capturing content for your audience, or a photographer working on your portfolio, hotel photography is all about creating a story. The best photos capture the essence of a hotel—the luxurious comfort of the rooms, the uniqueness of its amenities, and the ambiance of its public spaces—all while enticing potential guests to book a stay.
Taking professional-grade hotel photos involves more than just snapping pictures of rooms and hallways. It requires a keen eye for detail, an understanding of lighting, and an ability to compose shots that not only look beautiful but also evoke emotions. The challenge is to showcase a space in a way that feels inviting and authentic, drawing viewers into the experience. Achieving this takes preparation, creativity, and a clear vision of what makes the hotel unique.

In this guide, we’ll walk you through comprehensive tips to help you get the best shots during your hotel photoshoot. From planning your shoot and selecting the right gear to mastering natural light and capturing small details, these strategies will help you elevate your hotel photography to a professional level. Whether you’re shooting a sleek urban hotel or a tranquil beachside resort, these tips will ensure your photos highlight the best features and tell a captivating story. Let’s dive in!
Scout the Location in Advance
One of the most important steps in planning your hotel photoshoot is scouting the location in advance. Take time to explore every area of the hotel, from the lobby and rooms to outdoor spaces and amenities. Not only does this help you identify unique features, but it also allows you to evaluate lighting conditions, potential angles, and backdrops.
  • Interior Spaces: Check out guest rooms, suites, restaurants, bars, and lounges. Each space might have its own character, design theme, or special feature (such as a scenic view or high-end furniture) that deserves focus.
  • Exterior Spaces: Don’t forget to explore outdoor areas like the pool, garden, or rooftop bar, which often provide opportunities for stunning shots of the hotel’s surroundings or skyline views.

Tip: Take note of the lighting at different times of the day. This can affect the mood of your photos—bright and airy for daytime, or moody and intimate for evenings. Consider revisiting key spots during different hours to capture the perfect light.
Capture the Hotel's Unique Atmosphere
Every hotel has its own vibe, whether it's a luxurious five-star resort, a quirky boutique hotel, or a laid-back beach retreat. Reflecting the ambiance and overall experience is key to creating an emotional connection through your photos. You want potential guests to feel as if they’re already there, enjoying the space.
  • Luxury Hotels: Focus on high-end features like marble floors, plush bedding, chandeliers, and sophisticated décor. Use soft lighting and elegant angles to exude a sense of opulence and comfort.
  • Boutique Hotels: Highlight quirky, personalized touches such as artisanal furniture, colorful artwork, or cozy reading nooks. These small details often set boutique hotels apart.
  • Resorts and Beach Hotels: Emphasize leisure and relaxation by featuring pools, sun loungers, cabanas, and ocean views. Natural light and outdoor spaces are key to conveying a sense of serenity.

Tip: Pay attention to the design theme and stay consistent with your editing style to preserve the hotel’s identity.
Use Professional-Grade Equipment for Best Results
Investing in high-quality gear is a must if you’re aiming for professional-level shots. While smartphones can capture decent images, professional cameras give you more control over image quality, especially in challenging lighting conditions.
  • Cameras: A DSLR or mirrorless camera offers great versatility with manual settings to control exposure, white balance, and focus.
  • Lenses: A wide-angle lens (16mm–35mm range) is essential for capturing interior spaces without distortion. For detailed shots of design elements, use a macro or prime lens with a wider aperture (f/1.8 to f/2.8) to create stunning depth-of-field effects.
  • Accessories: A sturdy tripod is crucial for avoiding camera shake, particularly when shooting in low light. A remote shutter or self-timer can help prevent blurring, especially for long exposure shots.

Tip: Use portable lighting equipment or reflectors to ensure even illumination, especially in dimly lit areas like hallways or indoor lounges. Soft, diffused light works best for natural-looking photos.
Master Natural Light and Artificial Light
The quality and direction of light can make or break a photo, especially in hotel photography where ambiance is key. Here are some lighting tips to enhance your shots:
  • Natural Light: The best time to photograph interiors is during the day when natural light floods through windows, creating a soft, inviting glow. Open all curtains and blinds to allow maximum light in. For rooms without much natural light, position yourself near the window to make the most of what’s available.
  • Golden Hour: The hour after sunrise and the hour before sunset are ideal for photographing exteriors and outdoor spaces. The light during these times is soft and golden, creating beautiful warm tones and long, elegant shadows.
  • Artificial Light: If you must use indoor lighting, make sure to adjust the white balance on your camera to avoid yellow or blue color casts. Turn off ceiling lights or harsh overhead lighting to minimize unwanted shadows.

Tip: Combine natural and artificial lighting strategically to create layers of light in the frame, adding dimension to your shots.
Tell a Compelling Visual Story
Great hotel photography doesn’t just showcase beautiful spaces—it tells a story that evokes emotion and inspires action. Think about the entire guest journey, from arrival to check-out, and how you can illustrate each step in your photos.
  • Arrival Experience: Capture the grandeur of the lobby or the warmth of a welcoming front desk.
  • Relaxation: Show guests lounging by the pool, enjoying a spa day, or indulging in room service.
  • Dining: Photograph meals being served in the hotel restaurant, with beautifully arranged plates, soft candlelight, and inviting table settings.

Tip: Human elements like hands holding a drink, someone reading a book, or guests enjoying their stay can make your photos feel more personal and immersive.
Frame Your Shots with Intention
Composition is one of the most critical aspects of professional photography. For hotel photos, thoughtful framing can significantly enhance the appeal of your images. Here’s how to perfect it:
  • Rule of Thirds: Use this guideline to balance your shots. Position key elements along the lines or intersections in the frame to draw attention to focal points like a well-made bed, a balcony view, or a feature wall.
  • Leading Lines: Use architectural elements such as hallways, windows, or furniture arrangements to guide the viewer’s eye deeper into the image, creating a sense of depth and perspective.
  • Symmetry and Balance: Pay attention to symmetry when photographing rooms and spaces, especially in luxury hotels. A perfectly centered shot of a bed or hallway can convey a sense of order and calm.

Tip: Declutter the scene. Remove distractions like loose cables, newspapers, or open closet doors to maintain a clean, polished look.
Highlight Key Amenities and Unique Features
Hotels often attract guests through their special features and amenities. From lavish infinity pools to high-tech gym facilities, these areas deserve special attention. Make sure to capture these elements creatively to help potential guests envision themselves enjoying these experiences.
  • Outdoor Spaces: Capture wide shots of pools, courtyards, or terraces during the day, as well as moody, intimate evening shots with lights reflected in the water.
  • Spa and Wellness Areas: Soft lighting, close-ups of spa treatments, or a bubbling hot tub can make wellness facilities look irresistible.
  • Dining: If the hotel has an on-site restaurant or bar, food and drink photography can set the scene for a luxurious dining experience. Capture plates from overhead or at an angle that highlights the depth and texture of the dishes.

Tip: Use a mix of wide-angle shots and detail-oriented close-ups to give a full view of the amenities while highlighting special touches that stand out.
Edit Photos for Consistency and Professionalism
Post-production is where you can turn good photos into great ones. Consistent editing across all your images will create a cohesive portfolio that reflects the brand of the hotel.
  • Adjust Exposure: Ensure all your photos are evenly lit and that no details are lost in shadows or highlights.
  • Enhance Colors: Boost vibrancy without oversaturating. Keep colors true to life, especially in photos of room interiors, fabrics, or food.
  • Sharpen and Refine: Make sure your photos are sharp and clear. Pay attention to small details and clean up any imperfections in the scene, such as dust or stray objects.

Tip: Create presets in Adobe Lightroom or similar software to apply a uniform look and feel to all your photos with just one click.
Don’t Forget Nighttime Photography
Hotels often look magical at night, with warm interior lighting spilling into cozy rooms and outdoor spaces glowing under string lights or lanterns. Capturing the nighttime vibe of a hotel can add an alluring dimension to your portfolio.
  • Exterior Shots: Capture the hotel façade or outdoor dining areas lit up in the evening.
  • Long Exposures: For stunning nighttime shots, use long exposure to capture the movement of city lights, starry skies, or the glow of a candlelit dinner.

Tip: Use a tripod and adjust your camera settings to a lower ISO and longer shutter speed to capture sharp images in low light.
Always Shoot in RAW Format
Shooting in RAW format gives you maximum flexibility in post-production, allowing you to recover details in highlights and shadows and adjust white balance more effectively. This is especially important in hotel photography, where lighting conditions can vary dramatically from room to room.
By incorporating these detailed tips into your approach, you’ll be well-prepared to capture breathtaking, professional-quality hotel photos that tell a story, enhance the hotel’s brand, and create an emotional connection with your audience. Whether you're shooting for a hotel marketing campaign or showcasing travel experiences, these techniques will ensure your photos stand out.
Legal Considerations
Hotels must also consider legal aspects when it comes to photography. It is important to obtain permission from guests before using their photos for marketing purposes. Additionally, hotels must ensure that they have the necessary rights and licenses for any photos used on their website or marketing materials.
Hotels must also be aware of copyright laws when using photos taken by professional photographers. It is recommended to have a written agreement with the photographer outlining the usage rights of the photos.

Overall, understanding hotel photography is crucial for hotels to showcase their brand and attract potential guests. By hiring a professional photographer and choosing the right photography style, hotels can create high-quality photos that accurately represent their brand and amenities.
